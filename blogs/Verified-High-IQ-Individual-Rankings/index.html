RANK #3

Professor Terence Tao

IQ 230

Los Angeles, California, USA

VERIFICATION DETAILS

Estimated IQ: 230 (widely reported in academic literature)

Child Assessment: SAT Math 760 at age 8 (Johns Hopkins Study of Exceptional Talent)

Recognition: Fields Medal recipient, MacArthur Fellow

Institution: University of California, Los Angeles

BIOGRAPHICAL OVERVIEW

Professor Terence Chi-Shen Tao is an Australian-American mathematician widely regarded as one of the greatest living mathematicians. He holds the James and Carol Collins Chair in the College of Letters and Sciences at UCLA, where he has been a professor since 1996. Born in Adelaide to Chinese immigrant parents, Tao exhibited extraordinary mathematical abilities from early childhood, attending university-level mathematics courses at age nine.

Tao's prodigious talent was recognized through the Johns Hopkins Study of Exceptional Talent program, where at age eight he achieved a score of 760 on the SAT mathematics section—one of only three children in the program's history to score 700 or above at such a young age. He became the youngest gold medalist in International Mathematical Olympiad history at age thirteen. By sixteen, he had completed both bachelor's and master's degrees from Flinders University, and received his PhD from Princeton University under Elias Stein at age twenty-one.

In 1999, at age twenty-four, Tao became the youngest person ever promoted to full professor at UCLA. His research spans harmonic analysis, partial differential equations, algebraic and arithmetic combinatorics, geometric combinatorics, probability theory, compressed sensing, and analytic number theory. Colleagues describe him as having an "other-worldly ingenuity" and compare him to Mozart, noting that "mathematics just flows out of him." His collaborative approach has led to partnerships with over sixty-eight co-authors, and he has published more than three hundred research papers.

KEY ACHIEVEMENTS

  • Fields Medal (2006) for contributions to partial differential equations, combinatorics, harmonic analysis, and additive number theory
  • MacArthur Fellowship (2007), Breakthrough Prize in Mathematics (2014), Royal Medal (2014), Crafoord Prize (2012)
  • Proved the Green-Tao theorem with Ben Green, demonstrating that prime numbers contain arbitrarily long arithmetic progressions
  • Fellow of the Royal Society, Australian Academy of Science, National Academy of Sciences, American Mathematical Society
  • Youngest full professor in UCLA history (age 24) and youngest Fields Medal winner from Australia
  • Served on President's Council of Advisors on Science and Technology (2020-2024)

Estimated IQ: 225 (widely reported in scientific literature)

Specialization: Cosmology and astrophysics

BIOGRAPHICAL OVERVIEW

Christopher Hirata is an American cosmologist and astrophysicist. At the age of 13, he won the gold medal at the International Physics Olympiad, becoming the youngest American to do so. By age 14, he began university studies at the California Institute of Technology (Caltech). In 2001, at age 16, he began working with NASA on a project investigating the colonization of Mars.

He received his PhD in Physics from Princeton University in 2005 at age 22, under the supervision of Uroš Seljak. His thesis, "Weak Gravitational Lensing Theory and Data Analysis," laid groundwork for understanding dark energy. He is currently a professor at Ohio State University's Center for Cosmology and AstroParticle Physics (CCAPP).

Hirata is known for his work in pre-reionization helium, the generation of gravitational waves, and the theory of weak gravitational lensing. His cognitive capabilities are often cited as a benchmark for processing speed and theoretical synthesis in astrophysics.

BIOGRAPHICAL OVERVIEW

Kim Ung-Yong is a South Korean civil engineer and former child prodigy. He held the Guinness World Record for the highest IQ (210) for many years. His cognitive development was remarkably accelerated; he began speaking at 6 months, and by age 3, he was able to read Korean, Japanese, German, and English, as well as solve complex calculus problems.

At age 8, he was invited by NASA to the United States, where he worked as a researcher for ten years. During this time, he also audited university courses. Despite his massive intellectual potential and the expectations placed upon him, Kim chose to leave NASA and return to South Korea in 1978 to pursue a "normal life," eventually earning a PhD in Civil Engineering.

He is currently a professor at Shinhan University. Kim has frequently criticized the public's obsession with IQ scores, stating, "IQ is just another talent... having a high IQ doesn't mean you are omnipotent." His life serves as a case study in the psychological pressure placed on high-cognitive individuals.
